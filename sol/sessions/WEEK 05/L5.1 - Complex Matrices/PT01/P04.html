1. Complex Numbers and the Complex Vector Space \(\mathbb{C}^n\)

Definition

A complex number is an expression of the form \(z = a + ib\) where \(a, b \in \mathbb{R}\) and \(i = \sqrt{-1}\). The set of all complex numbers is denoted by \(\mathbb{C}\). The \(n\)-dimensional complex Euclidean space \(\mathbb{C}^n\) is the set of all ordered \(n\)-tuples \((x_1, x_2, \dots, x_n)\) with each \(x_j \in \mathbb{C}\).

Explanation

Complex numbers arise naturally when solving polynomial equations. For example, \(x^2 + 1 = 0\) has no real solution but has solutions \(x = i\) and \(x = -i\) in \(\mathbb{C}\). In machine learning and linear algebra, complex numbers appear in Fourier transforms, quantum computing, signal processing, and in the analysis of matrices with complex eigenvalues.

The space \(\mathbb{C}^n\) is the complex counterpart of the real Euclidean space \(\mathbb{R}^n\). Vectors in \(\mathbb{C}^n\) can be added componentwise: if \(x = (x_1,\dots,x_n)\) and \(y = (y_1,\dots,y_n)\), then \(x+y = (x_1+y_1,\dots,x_n+y_n)\). Scalar multiplication by a complex scalar \(\alpha \in \mathbb{C}\) is defined as \(\alpha x = (\alpha x_1,\dots,\alpha x_n)\). With these operations, \(\mathbb{C}^n\) is a vector space over the field \(\mathbb{C}\).

Important operations on a single complex number include the complex conjugate: if \(z = a+ib\), then \(\bar{z} = a - ib\). In polar form, \(z = r e^{i\theta}\) with \(r = |z| = \sqrt{a^2+b^2}\) and \(\theta = \arg(z)\), and \(\bar{z} = r e^{-i\theta}\). The conjugate has the properties \(\overline{z_1+z_2} = \bar{z_1}+\bar{z_2}\), \(\overline{z_1 z_2} = \bar{z_1}\bar{z_2}\), and \(\bar{\bar{z}} = z\).

Intuition / Mental Model

Think of \(\mathbb{C}^n\) as \(\mathbb{R}^{2n}\) with an extra algebraic structure: each complex coordinate has a real part and an imaginary part. But the scalar multiplication allows rotation and scaling in a way that is not available in real vector spaces. The conjugate operation reflects a point across the real axis in the complex plane.

Key Definitions

  • Complex number \(z = a+ib\): \(a = \operatorname{Re}(z)\) is the real part, \(b = \operatorname{Im}(z)\) is the imaginary part.
  • Complex conjugate \(\bar{z}\): \(a-ib\); geometrically reflection about the real axis.
  • Modulus (absolute value) \(|z|\): \(\sqrt{a^2+b^2} = \sqrt{z\bar{z}}\).
  • \(\mathbb{C}^n\): set of all column vectors with \(n\) complex entries.

Formulas

\[ \begin{aligned} (a+ib)+(c+id) &= (a+c) + i(b+d), \\ (a+ib)(c+id) &= (ac-bd) + i(ad+bc), \\ \overline{a+ib} &= a-ib, \\ |a+ib| &= \sqrt{a^2+b^2}. \end{aligned} \]

Worked Example: Let \(z_1 = 2+3i\) and \(z_2 = 1-4i\). Then \(z_1+z_2 = 3 - i\). \(z_1 z_2 = (2)(1) - (3)(-4) + i[(2)(-4)+(3)(1)] = 2+12 + i(-8+3)=14 -5i\). \(\bar{z_1}=2-3i\). \(|z_1| = \sqrt{4+9}=\sqrt{13}\).

Common Mistakes

  • Misconception: "The conjugate of a product is the product of the conjugates, but the conjugate of a sum is not the sum of conjugates."
    (Correction: The conjugate distributes over both addition and multiplication: \(\overline{z_1+z_2}=\bar{z_1}+\bar{z_2}\) and \(\overline{z_1z_2}=\bar{z_1}\bar{z_2}\).)
  • Misconception: "\(|z|^2 = z^2\) for any complex \(z\)."
    (Correction: \(z^2\) is not generally real; \(|z|^2 = z\bar{z}\) is always real and non-negative.)

Edge Cases

  • If \(z\) is real (\(b=0\)), then \(\bar{z}=z\) and \(|z|^2 = z^2\).
  • The zero vector in \(\mathbb{C}^n\) has all entries \(0+0i\); it is the additive identity.

Real-world Application

In quantum mechanics, the state of a qubit is represented by a vector in \(\mathbb{C}^2\) with unit norm. In digital signal processing, the Fourier transform maps real signals to complex frequency domain vectors in \(\mathbb{C}^n\).

Key Takeaways

* \(\mathbb{C}^n\) is a vector space over \(\mathbb{C}\); addition and scalar multiplication are componentwise.
* Complex conjugation is an involution (applying twice returns the original).
* The modulus \(|z|\) is a norm on \(\mathbb{C}\) satisfying triangle inequality and multiplicativity.
* Real numbers are a subset of \(\mathbb{C}\) with \(b=0\).
* Every complex number can be represented as \(r e^{i\theta}\) (polar form), which is essential for understanding eigenvalues of unitary matrices.

2. Inner Product (Dot Product) for Complex Vectors

Definition

For vectors \(x = (x_1,\dots,x_n)^T\) and \(y = (y_1,\dots,y_n)^T\) in \(\mathbb{C}^n\), the standard inner product (also called the Hermitian inner product) is defined as

\[ \langle x, y \rangle = x \cdot y = \bar{x}^T y = \sum_{i=1}^n \bar{x}_i y_i. \]

Explanation

In \(\mathbb{R}^n\), the dot product \(x^T y = \sum x_i y_i\) works perfectly. However, if we naively extend this to \(\mathbb{C}^n\), we get \(\sum x_i y_i\) which fails to define a positive length because a non-zero vector could have zero “length”. For example, take \(x = (1, i)\) in \(\mathbb{C}^2\). Then \(x^T x = 1^2 + i^2 = 1 -1 = 0\). This violates the requirement that a norm should be zero only for the zero vector. Moreover, the usual dot product in \(\mathbb{C}^n\) would not be conjugate symmetric, which is necessary for a Hermitian inner product.

The corrected definition \(\langle x,y \rangle = \sum \bar{x}_i y_i\) guarantees that \(\langle x,x \rangle = \sum |x_i|^2 \ge 0\) and equals zero only when all \(x_i = 0\). This is called a positive-definite Hermitian form. The conjugate on the first argument ensures conjugate symmetry: \(\langle x,y \rangle = \overline{\langle y,x \rangle}\).

The inner product satisfies the following properties for all vectors \(x,y,z \in \mathbb{C}^n\) and scalars \(\alpha \in \mathbb{C}\):

  • Conjugate symmetry: \(\langle x,y \rangle = \overline{\langle y,x \rangle}\).
  • Linearity in the second argument: \(\langle x, \alpha y + z \rangle = \alpha \langle x,y \rangle + \langle x,z \rangle\).
  • Conjugate linearity in the first argument: \(\langle \alpha x + z, y \rangle = \bar{\alpha} \langle x,y \rangle + \langle z,y \rangle\).
  • Positive definiteness: \(\langle x,x \rangle \ge 0\) and \(\langle x,x \rangle = 0 \iff x = 0\).

Intuition / Mental Model

The inner product measures the “alignment” between two complex vectors, but the conjugation makes the length real and positive. Think of it as taking the dot product after reflecting the first vector across the real axis in each coordinate.

Key Definitions

  • Inner product \(\langle x,y \rangle\): a complex-valued function satisfying the four properties above.
  • Orthogonal vectors: \(x \perp y\) if \(\langle x,y \rangle = 0\).
  • Norm (length): \(\|x\| = \sqrt{\langle x,x \rangle} = \sqrt{\sum |x_i|^2}\).

Formulas

\[ \langle x,y \rangle = \bar{x}^T y = \sum_{i=1}^n \bar{x}_i y_i, \qquad \|x\| = \sqrt{\langle x,x \rangle} = \sqrt{\sum_{i=1}^n |x_i|^2}. \]

Worked Example: Let \(x = (1+i, \; 2-3i)^T\) and \(y = (4, \; -i)^T\). Then \(\bar{x} = (1-i, \; 2+3i)\). Compute \(\langle x,y \rangle = (1-i)\cdot 4 + (2+3i)\cdot (-i) = 4-4i + (2+3i)(-i) = 4-4i + [-2i -3i^2] = 4-4i + (-2i +3) = 7 -6i\). The norm of \(x\): \(\|x\| = \sqrt{|1+i|^2 + |2-3i|^2} = \sqrt{(1^2+1^2) + (2^2+(-3)^2)} = \sqrt{2+13}=\sqrt{15}\).

Common Mistakes

  • Misconception: "The inner product is symmetric: \(\langle x,y \rangle = \langle y,x \rangle\) for complex vectors."
    (Correction: It is conjugate symmetric: \(\langle x,y \rangle = \overline{\langle y,x \rangle}\). For real vectors, this reduces to symmetry because conjugates do nothing.)
  • Misconception: "Scalars can be pulled out of the first argument without conjugation."
    (Correction: \(\langle \alpha x, y \rangle = \bar{\alpha} \langle x,y \rangle\). This is a common source of sign errors in calculations.)
  • Misconception: "\(\langle x,y \rangle = x^T y\) works fine for complex vectors."
    (Correction: That definition fails positive definiteness, as shown by \(x=(1,i)\). The correct definition uses the conjugate transpose.)

Edge Cases

  • If \(x\) and \(y\) are real vectors, \(\langle x,y \rangle = x^T y\), the usual dot product.
  • The Cauchy–Schwarz inequality holds: \(|\langle x,y \rangle| \le \|x\|\|y\|\), with equality iff \(x\) and \(y\) are linearly dependent.
  • If \(\langle x,y \rangle = 0\), the vectors are orthogonal even if they are complex.

Real-world Application

In wireless communications, the inner product of a received signal vector with a known code vector determines the correlation and helps in decoding. The complex inner product naturally handles in-phase and quadrature components.

Key Takeaways

* The complex inner product conjugates the first vector's components to ensure positivity of the norm.
* \(\langle x,y \rangle = \bar{x}^T y\) is the standard definition used in quantum mechanics, signal processing, and advanced linear algebra.
* The norm \(\|x\| = \sqrt{\sum |x_i|^2}\) is always a non-negative real number.
* Orthogonality in \(\mathbb{C}^n\) is defined via \(\langle x,y \rangle = 0\).
* Exam tip: Always conjugate the first vector when computing inner products in \(\mathbb{C}^n\).

3. Conjugate Transpose (Hermitian Adjoint) of a Matrix

Definition

Let \(A\) be an \(m \times n\) matrix with complex entries. The conjugate transpose (also called Hermitian transpose or adjoint) of \(A\), denoted \(A^*\) or \(A^H\), is the \(n \times m\) matrix obtained by taking the transpose of \(A\) and then taking the complex conjugate of every entry. Equivalently, \((A^*)_{ij} = \overline{A_{ji}}\).

Explanation

The conjugate transpose generalises the transpose operation from real matrices to complex matrices. For real matrices, \(A^* = A^T\) because conjugation does nothing. The adjoint plays a central role in defining Hermitian and unitary matrices, and in the spectral theorem.

Properties of the conjugate transpose (valid for all matrices of compatible sizes and scalars \(\alpha \in \mathbb{C}\)):

  • \((A^*)^* = A\) (involution).
  • \((\alpha A)^* = \bar{\alpha} A^*\).
  • \((A+B)^* = A^* + B^*\).
  • \((AB)^* = B^* A^*\) (reversal rule).
  • If \(A\) is invertible, then \((A^*)^{-1} = (A^{-1})^*\).
  • \(\langle Ax, y \rangle = \langle x, A^* y \rangle\) for any \(x \in \mathbb{C}^n, y \in \mathbb{C}^m\).

Note: The order of operations (transpose then conjugate) yields the same result as conjugate then transpose.

Intuition / Mental Model

Think of \(A^*\) as the “complex version” of the transpose: reflect the matrix across its main diagonal and then flip the sign of the imaginary parts of all entries.

Key Definitions

  • Conjugate transpose \(A^*\): \((\overline{A})^T = \overline{A^T}\).
  • Hermitian matrix: a square matrix \(A\) such that \(A^* = A\).
  • Unitary matrix: a square matrix \(U\) such that \(U^* U = U U^* = I\).

Formulas

\[ A = \begin{pmatrix} a_{11} & a_{12} \\ a_{21} & a_{22} \end{pmatrix} \quad\Rightarrow\quad A^* = \begin{pmatrix} \bar{a}_{11} & \bar{a}_{21} \\ \bar{a}_{12} & \bar{a}_{22} \end{pmatrix}. \]

Worked Example: Let \(A = \begin{pmatrix} 1+i & 3-2i \\ 2-4i & i \end{pmatrix}\). Then first take the transpose: \(A^T = \begin{pmatrix} 1+i & 2-4i \\ 3-2i & i \end{pmatrix}\). Then conjugate each entry: \(\overline{A^T} = \begin{pmatrix} 1-i & 2+4i \\ 3+2i & -i \end{pmatrix}\). Thus \(A^* = \begin{pmatrix} 1-i & 2+4i \\ 3+2i & -i \end{pmatrix}\). Note that we could conjugate first: \(\overline{A} = \begin{pmatrix} 1-i & 3+2i \\ 2+4i & -i \end{pmatrix}\), then transpose gives the same result.

Common Mistakes

  • Misconception: "\((AB)^* = A^* B^*\)"
    (Correction: The correct reversal rule is \((AB)^* = B^* A^*\). This is crucial for deriving properties of unitary and Hermitian matrices.)
  • Misconception: "For a complex scalar \(\alpha\), \((\alpha A)^* = \alpha A^*\)."
    (Correction: The scalar is conjugated: \((\alpha A)^* = \bar{\alpha} A^*\).)
  • Misconception: "The conjugate transpose of a real matrix is just the transpose."
    (Correction: Correct, because conjugation does nothing on reals. This is fine.)

Edge Cases

  • If \(A\) is a column vector \(x \in \mathbb{C}^n\), then \(x^*\) is a row vector with conjugated entries.
  • If \(A\) is a row vector, \(A^*\) is a column vector with conjugated entries.
  • For a diagonal matrix \(D = \operatorname{diag}(d_1,\dots,d_n)\), we have \(D^* = \operatorname{diag}(\bar{d}_1,\dots,\bar{d}_n)\).

Real-world Application

In beamforming and array signal processing, the conjugate transpose of the steering vector is used to compute the spatial correlation matrix. In quantum computing, the adjoint of a unitary gate is its inverse, which is essential for reversible computation.

Key Takeaways

* The conjugate transpose combines transposition and complex conjugation.
* \(A^*\) satisfies the reversal rule \((AB)^* = B^* A^*\).
* For any vectors \(x,y\), \(\langle Ax, y \rangle = \langle x, A^* y \rangle\).
* Hermitian matrices satisfy \(A^* = A\); unitary matrices satisfy \(U^* = U^{-1}\).
* Exam tip: Always compute \(A^*\) by transposing first then conjugating (or vice versa) to avoid mixing indices.

4. Hermitian Matrices

Definition

A square matrix \(A \in \mathbb{C}^{n \times n}\) is called Hermitian (or self-adjoint) if it equals its own conjugate transpose: \(A^* = A\). In other words, \(A_{ij} = \overline{A_{ji}}\) for all \(i,j\).

Explanation

Hermitian matrices are the complex analogue of real symmetric matrices. For a real matrix, Hermitian means symmetric because conjugation does nothing. For complex matrices, Hermitian forces the diagonal entries to be real (since \(A_{ii} = \overline{A_{ii}}\) implies \(A_{ii} \in \mathbb{R}\)), and the off-diagonal entries come in complex conjugate pairs: \(A_{ij} = \overline{A_{ji}}\).

Hermitian matrices possess extremely nice properties:

  • All eigenvalues are real.
  • Eigenvectors corresponding to distinct eigenvalues are orthogonal (with respect to the complex inner product).
  • They are unitarily diagonalizable (spectral theorem).
  • The quadratic form \(x^* A x\) is real for all \(x \in \mathbb{C}^n\).

These properties make Hermitian matrices fundamental in quantum mechanics (observables are Hermitian), optimization (Hessian matrices), and machine learning (covariance matrices).

Intuition / Mental Model

Think of a Hermitian matrix as a “symmetric” matrix where the imaginary parts are antisymmetric: the entry above the diagonal is the complex conjugate of the entry below, ensuring that the matrix is not skewed in the complex plane.

Key Definitions

  • Hermitian matrix: \(A^* = A\).
  • Skew-Hermitian matrix: \(A^* = -A\).
  • Positive definite Hermitian: \(x^* A x > 0\) for all non-zero \(x\).

Formulas

\[ A = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 3-3i \\ 3+3i & 5 \end{pmatrix} \]

Check: \(A^* = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 3+3i \\ 3-3i & 5 \end{pmatrix}^T?\) Actually compute carefully: Conjugate of A: \(\overline{A} = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 3+3i \\ 3-3i & 5 \end{pmatrix}\). Transpose gives \(\begin{pmatrix} 2 & 3-3i \\ 3+3i & 5 \end{pmatrix} = A\). So \(A\) is Hermitian. Diagonal entries 2 and 5 are real, off-diagonals are conjugates.

Worked Example (Eigenvalues of a Hermitian matrix):

Take \(A = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 3-3i \\ 3+3i & 5 \end{pmatrix}\). Characteristic polynomial: \(\det(A-\lambda I) = (2-\lambda)(5-\lambda) - (3-3i)(3+3i) = (2-\lambda)(5-\lambda) - (9+9) = (2-\lambda)(5-\lambda) - 18\). Expand: \(10 -2\lambda -5\lambda +\lambda^2 -18 = \lambda^2 -7\lambda -8 = (\lambda-8)(\lambda+1)\). Eigenvalues: 8 and -1 (both real).

Common Mistakes

  • Misconception: "A Hermitian matrix must have all entries real."
    (Correction: Only the diagonal entries must be real; off-diagonals can be complex as long as they are conjugates.)
  • Misconception: "If \(A\) is Hermitian, then \(A^T = A\) also holds."
    (Correction: For complex matrices, \(A^T = A\) is not the same as \(A^* = A\). The transpose condition is much stronger and forces all entries to be real; such matrices are called symmetric real matrices.)
  • Misconception: "Hermitian matrices are always diagonalizable over \(\mathbb{R}\)."
    (Correction: They are unitarily diagonalizable over \(\mathbb{C}\), and the diagonal entries (eigenvalues) are real, but the eigenvectors may be complex.)

Edge Cases

  • The zero matrix and the identity matrix are Hermitian.
  • Any real symmetric matrix is Hermitian.
  • If \(A\) is Hermitian and invertible, then \(A^{-1}\) is also Hermitian.
  • The sum of two Hermitian matrices is Hermitian; the product of two Hermitian matrices is Hermitian if and only if they commute.

Real-world Application

In quantum mechanics, every observable (like position, momentum, spin) is represented by a Hermitian operator. Its real eigenvalues are the possible measurement outcomes. In machine learning, the covariance matrix of real data is symmetric, hence Hermitian; its eigenvectors give principal components.

Key Takeaways

* Hermitian matrices are self-adjoint: \(A^* = A\).
* Diagonal entries are real; off-diagonals are complex conjugates.
* Eigenvalues are real; eigenvectors for distinct eigenvalues are orthogonal.
* Hermitian matrices are unitarily diagonalizable (spectral theorem).
* Exam tip: When checking if a matrix is Hermitian, verify \(A_{ii} \in \mathbb{R}\) and \(A_{ij} = \overline{A_{ji}}\) for \(i \neq j\).
